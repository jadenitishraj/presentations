00

Why agent eval is hard.

A classifier has one job and one truth: a label is right or wrong. An LLM agent has many right answers, none of them deterministic, produced by a pipeline of agents that each fail differently. You need more than accuracy.

Key vocabulary
DeterministicSame input always produces same output. Classical software. LLMs are not this.
Ground truthThe known-correct answer used to score a model. For free-form prose, it is rarely unique.
Lexical metricScores word overlap. BLEU, ROUGE. Fast, deterministic, blind to meaning.
Semantic metricScores meaning. Uses an LLM judge or embeddings. Slower, fuzzier, understands paraphrase.
Behavioral metricScores what the agent did, not just what it said. Tool calls, routing, completion.
End-to-end vs component evalScore only the final answer, or score each node in the graph independently.

There are three failure modes a single end-to-end accuracy number cannot distinguish, and a real eval suite has to separate them.

Wording failure

The answer is correct but phrased differently from the reference. Lexical metrics punish this. Semantic metrics let it through. Knowing which metric is firing matters.

Truth failure

The answer sounds confident but contradicts its sources or invents facts. Hallucination and faithfulness metrics live here. The most dangerous failure mode for a research agent.

Compliance failure

The answer is correct and grounded but exposes an Aadhaar number, scores a person, or pretends not to be an AI. Regulators care, quality metrics do not.

THE PRACTITIONER'S TAKEA complete agent evaluation answers four questions, not one. Did it say it well? (lexical) Did it say the truth? (hallucination/faithfulness) Did it do the right thing? (tool/routing) Was it safe to say? (compliance). Most teams measure question one and ship. That's why production agents surprise their owners.
Section summary

Agent evaluation is not classifier accuracy with a coat of paint. It is a portfolio of metrics across lexical, semantic, behavioral, and compliance axes — because an agent can fail on any of them while looking fine on the others. The rest of this lesson is that portfolio, one metric at a time.

01

BLEU. Did you add junk?

The original machine-translation metric, built at IBM in 2002. It scores precision over n-grams — of the words your model produced, how many appear in the reference. Still everywhere, often wrong.

Key vocabulary
BLEUBilingual Evaluation Understudy. Papineni et al., IBM, 2002. Originally for translation.
n-gramA run of n consecutive words. "the cat sat" is a 3-gram. BLEU usually averages 1- through 4-grams.
PrecisionOf what you produced, what fraction is correct. Answers "did you add junk?"
Reference (gold)The human-written correct answer that the model output is scored against.
Brevity penaltyA multiplier that punishes outputs shorter than the reference — stops models from gaming precision by writing one word.
SmoothingA small fix that prevents BLEU from collapsing to 0 when higher n-grams don't match. Critical for short sentences.

BLEU asks a single question: of the words my model wrote, how many appear in the reference? Higher is better. The reference is treated as gospel, and anything the model adds outside it is treated as junk.

The intuition (not the full formula)
BLEU ≈ brevity_penalty × geometric_mean( precision_1gram, precision_2gram, precision_3gram, precision_4gram )

Each precision_n = (n-grams in output that appear in reference) / (total n-grams in output)

Worked example · the cat sat

Reference: the cat sat on the mat
Model output: the cat sat on a mat

Model words: the · cat · sat · on · a · mat — 6 tokens. Five of them (the · cat · sat · on · mat) appear in the reference. One (a) does not.

Unigram precision = 5/6 ≈ 0.83. A high score, because the model added almost no junk.

memory hook

BLEU is the tight talker. It rewards you for staying inside the reference's vocabulary and punishes you for adding anything outside it. Did you add junk?

WHEN BLEU IS THE RIGHT TOOLMachine translation, structured output, short factual answers, code generation against a canonical reference. Anywhere the exact wording matters.
WHEN BLEU LIES TO YOUFree-form prose, summarization, creative writing, agent answers with multiple valid phrasings. BLEU has no concept of synonyms — "feline" and "cat" mean nothing to it.
SMOOTHING — WHY YOUR CODE NEEDS ITWithout smoothing, BLEU returns 0 the moment any 4-gram fails to match. On short agent answers, 4-gram matches are rare, so unsmoothed BLEU is mostly zero. Your lexical_code.py uses SmoothingFunction().method1 — that is the right call.
Section summary

BLEU is a precision-over-n-grams metric: of what the model said, how much overlapped the reference. It is fast, deterministic, and brutally literal. Use it for translation and short factual outputs. For your research agent's free-form prose, expect low scores even on correct answers — because BLEU cannot see meaning, only matching words.

02

ROUGE. Did you miss stuff?

Built for summarization (Lin, 2004). The mirror image of BLEU — it scores recall. Of the words in the reference, how many did your model capture?

Key vocabulary
ROUGERecall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation. Chin-Yew Lin, 2004. Built for summarization.
RecallOf the things that should be there, how many are. Answers "did you miss stuff?"
ROUGE-1 / ROUGE-2Overlap of single words (unigrams) or word pairs (bigrams) between output and reference.
ROUGE-LLongest Common Subsequence. Rewards getting word order roughly right, not just bag-of-words.
F-measureHarmonic mean of precision and recall. Balanced single score. Your code uses .fmeasure.
StemmerStrips suffixes so "running" matches "run". Softens the synonym problem a little — but not much.

If BLEU is the tight talker, ROUGE is the completeness auditor. It does not care how clean your output is — it cares whether everything in the reference made it into your output.

BLEU

Precision · translation
  • Asks: did you add words that aren't in the reference?
  • Penalizes extras and inventions
  • Built for machine translation
  • Score = how clean is the output
vs

ROUGE

Recall · summarization
  • Asks: did you cover everything in the reference?
  • Penalizes missing content
  • Built for text summarization
  • Score = how complete is the output

Worked example · same reference, opposite lens

Reference: the cat sat on the mat (6 words)
Model output: the cat sat on a mat

Reference words the model captured: the · cat · sat · on · mat — 5 of 6. Missed one instance of "the".

Recall = 5/6 ≈ 0.83. Same high score as BLEU here — because the two sentences are nearly identical. The difference shows up only when wording diverges.

memory hook

BLEU asks did you add junk? ROUGE asks did you miss stuff? Same overlap, opposite question.

YOUR CODE USES ROUGE-Llexical_code.py uses rougeL with use_stemmer=True. ROUGE-L matches the longest common word sequence — so "the cat sat on the mat" vs "the cat is on the mat" gets credit for the partial sequence, not just unordered overlap. The stemmer is a nice softener.
PRECISION + RECALL = F-MEASUREPure recall lets a model game the metric by dumping in everything. So ROUGE in practice reports F-measure — the harmonic mean of precision and recall — which is what your .fmeasure call extracts.
Section summary

ROUGE is recall-over-n-grams: of what the reference said, how much did the model capture. ROUGE-L specifically rewards correct word order via the longest common subsequence. Use it for summarization and any task where coverage matters more than terseness. Like BLEU, it sees words, not meaning — which sets up the lexical trap on the next page.

03

The lexical trap. Both metrics fail the same way.

BLEU and ROUGE share a blind spot. They count words. They do not understand them. The moment your model paraphrases — which is exactly what a good research agent should do — both scores collapse.

Key vocabulary
Synonym blindnessThe inability of lexical metrics to recognize that "cat" and "feline" mean the same thing.
ParaphraseSame meaning, different words. The natural output of any capable LLM.
Reference dependenceThe metric's quality is bounded by the quality and breadth of your reference. One canned reference, one wording allowed.
Semantic metricMetric that uses meaning, not strings. LLM-as-judge (G-Eval), embedding similarity, faithfulness. The fix for the lexical trap.

Here is the worked example that turns the lesson into a slide. Same meaning, different words. Watch what BLEU and ROUGE do.

Same meaning. Different words. Watch the scores collapse.

Model output: the cat sat on the mat

Any human reader says: identical meaning, perfectly correct answer. But the only words that match are function words — the · on · the. The content words (feline/cat, rested/sat, rug/mat) all differ.

≈ 0.30
Terrible — for a correct answer.
ROUGE-L score
≈ 0.30
Same trap, same number.
THE FAILURE MODE FOR YOUR AGENTYour research agent does live web search. It pulls current phrasing, structures answers its own way, adds context. Against a single canned expected_output reference, BLEU and ROUGE will score low even when the agent answer is better than the reference. That's not a bug. That's the metric's nature.
THE FIX — SEMANTIC METRICSThis is why DeepEval ships LLM-as-judge metrics: G-Eval, Faithfulness, Answer Relevancy. They run an LLM over the output and reference to compare meaning instead of words. They cost more (judge LLM calls) but understand paraphrase. Run BLEU/ROUGE alongside them, never alone.
Section summary

BLEU and ROUGE share a fatal weakness: they reward word overlap, not meaning. A correct paraphrase scores as low as a wrong answer. For free-form agent output they are necessary but never sufficient — you pair them with semantic metrics that understand language. Teach the contrast, never hide it.

Pass@k. Capability under a budget.

From the OpenAI Codex / HumanEval paper, 2021. Originally for code generation: given k attempts, did at least one work? The right way to measure non-deterministic generation — and the right way to justify a multi-query retrieval node.

Key vocabulary
Pass@kProbability that at least one of k independent samples passes the verifier. Single number per benchmark.
Verifier / oracleThe thing that says pass/fail per sample. Unit tests for code. Gold-chunk match for retrieval. Without it, no pass@k.
Independent sampleAn attempt drawn from the model without conditioning on previous attempts. Critical: sequential retries are not independent.
Best-of-k (inference technique)Generate k at runtime, pick a winner. Shares pass@k's shape — but is a technique, not the metric.
Gold chunkThe pre-labeled correct chunk in your vector store for a given test question. The verifier for RAG pass@k.
Capability vs reliabilityThe gap between pass@1 and pass@k tells you which one your model lacks.

Pass@k answers one question: if I let the model try k times, how often does it get it right at least once? It is a ruler, not a tool. It scores capability under a fixed budget.

The estimator (unbiased version)
pass@k = 1 − C(n−c, k) / C(n, k)

where n = total samples generated, c = number that passed, k = the budget you care about.

In words: one minus the probability that all k randomly drawn samples come from the failing pile.

Pass@k is one number, not a range.

A common mistake: thinking pass@k changes per question. It does not. You pick k upfront ("I'll let the model try 5 times") and run that fixed budget across every question in your benchmark. The result is one percentage. The only reason to compute pass@1, pass@5, pass@10 side by side is to draw a curve showing how much retrying helps.

the key distinction

Pass@k is the ruler — an evaluation metric. The tool that shares its shape is best-of-k sampling — an inference technique. The difference: evaluation gets an oracle to pick the winner for free. Production has to build one.

THE JUMP IS THE JUSTIFICATION+25 points of retrieval recall from generating 5 variants. That gap is the entire argument for your multi-query node existing. Without measuring it, the architecture is faith-based. With it, you have a number defensible in a design review.
WHY IT DOESN'T FIT YOUR WRITER-CRITIC LOOPPass@k needs parallel, independent samples with an oracle verifier. Your Writer-Critic loop is sequential (each draft conditioned on previous critique), dependent, and uses a soft LLM Critic, not an oracle. Plus you ship the last draft, not the best of k. For the loop, the right metric is iterations-to-acceptance, not pass@k.
THE GOLD-LABEL COSTPass@k is not free. You need a small benchmark — 15 to 20 questions — each tagged with its correct chunk ID in your vector store. Build it once, run it forever. Synthetic option: pick a chunk, ask an LLM to write a question that chunk would answer. Now you know the gold.
Section summary

Pass@k is the metric for parallel, independent attempts against a hard verifier. It does not fit sequential refinement loops — those need iterations-to-acceptance instead. Its perfect home in your project is the multi-query RAG node, where comparing pass@1 to pass@5 quantifies the value of query expansion in one number. The gap between pass@1 and pass@k is the architectural proof.

05

The retrieval trio. Depth vs breadth.

Three retrieval metrics, three different questions. Precision and Recall zoom into one query. Pass@k zooms out across many. Together they tell the full story.

Key vocabulary
Context PrecisionOf the chunks I returned, how many are actually relevant? Measures cleanliness.
Context RecallOf the relevant chunks that exist, how many did I find? Measures coverage.
Pass@kAcross k query attempts, did any succeed? Measures the value of trying multiple times.
F1Harmonic mean of precision and recall. Single balanced score.
RAGASPopular framework that ships Context Precision and Context Recall out of the box. Uses LLM-as-judge.
MRR / NDCGPosition-aware retrieval metrics — reward putting the right chunk near the top, not just in the result set.

Setup for the example below: question has 4 gold chunks. Retrieval returns 5 chunks. Of those 5, 3 are actually gold, 2 are junk.

Metric Question it answers Formula This example
Recall How many of the right chunks did I find? gold found / total gold 3 / 4 = 0.75
Precision Of what I returned, how much is right? gold found / total returned 3 / 5 = 0.60
Pass@k Did any of my k query attempts hit a gold? binary per question, averaged across set 0 or 1 per Q
PRECISION AND RECALL TRADE OFFReturn only the top-1 chunk and it's gold? Precision = 1.0, recall might be 0.25. Return all 1000 chunks in your DB? Recall = 1.0, precision tanks. Always report both, or combine them as F1.
PASS@K IS A DIFFERENT AXISPrecision and recall measure the quality of one retrieval call. Pass@k measures the value of trying again. Same retriever, k=1 vs k=5, lets you isolate whether multi-query expansion is earning its keep.
PASS@K IS OPTIMISTICIt doesn't care which query succeeded or how clean its results were. A query that returns the gold chunk plus 50 garbage chunks still "passes." Pass@k assumes downstream re-ranking or the LLM will pick the gold from noise. Pair it with precision for honest reporting.
Section summary

Precision measures cleanliness, Recall measures coverage, Pass@k measures the breadth of trying multiple queries. Report all three when defending a RAG design. None alone is enough: a system with high recall but low precision wastes tokens; one with high precision but low pass@k cannot exploit query diversity. The trio is the honest answer.

Hallucination & faithfulness. Did it lie. Did it stay grounded.

The two semantic metrics that matter most for a research agent. Both LLM-as-judge. Both in your hallucination.py. They sound similar — and DeepEval keeps them separate on purpose.

Key vocabulary
HallucinationOutput contains statements that contradict the provided context. Lower score = better. Threshold is a maximum.
FaithfulnessOutput's claims are all supported by the retrieval context. Higher score = better. Threshold is a minimum.
Claim decompositionThe judge LLM breaks the output into atomic factual statements, then scores each.
Context (ground truth)What HallucinationMetric treats as truth. Ideally a curated gold context, not just retrieved snippets.
Retrieval contextWhat FaithfulnessMetric checks against — the actual chunks your RAG pulled. Different concept from context.
LLM-as-judgeThe validation engine for both. A separate LLM (gpt-4o-mini in your code) reads output + context and scores.

Same scenario, four different agent outputs. Watch which metric catches which failure.

User asks: "When was the Eiffel Tower built?"

Retrieved context: "The Eiffel Tower was completed in 1889 in Paris, designed by Gustave Eiffel for the World's Fair."

Case 01 · Grounded
Perfectly faithful
"The Eiffel Tower was completed in 1889."
Halluc0.0 Faith1.0
Case 02 · Contradicts
Classic hallucination
"The Eiffel Tower was completed in 1875 by Gustave Eiffel."
Halluc0.5 Faith0.5
Case 03 · Adds unsupported
The silent failure
"The Eiffel Tower was completed in 1889 and is 324 meters tall."
Halluc0.0 Faith0.5
Case 04 · Paraphrased
Different words, same facts
"Gustave Eiffel's tower was finished in 1889 for the World's Fair in Paris."
Halluc0.0 Faith1.0
CASE 03 IS THE KILLERThe agent added a true-sounding fact (the height actually is 324m) — but it's not from the source. Hallucination misses it (no contradiction). Faithfulness catches it (not supported). This is exactly why DeepEval keeps the two metrics separate. The dangerous failure mode in a research agent is not lying — it's confidently sneaking in details the sources never mentioned.
PARAPHRASE PASSES BOTHCase 04 reorders and rephrases — and both metrics give it full marks. That is the contrast with BLEU/ROUGE: those would have tanked the same output. Semantic metrics see meaning, lexical metrics see strings.

The threshold asymmetry — easy to get wrong.

Hallucination

Lower is better · max threshold
  • Score is the fraction of contexts the output contradicts
  • 0.0 = perfectly clean; 1.0 = contradicts everything
  • Threshold is a ceiling — passes if score ≤ threshold
  • Your code: threshold=0.3 — at most 30% contradiction allowed
vs

Faithfulness

Higher is better · min threshold
  • Score is the fraction of claims supported by retrieval context
  • 1.0 = every claim grounded; 0.0 = nothing supported
  • Threshold is a floor — passes if score ≥ threshold
  • Your code: threshold=0.6 — lenient; 0.8 is the production bar

How validation actually happens — the judge mechanism.

  1. 1.
    Your agent produces a final answer. Searcher fetches snippets, Writer drafts prose. Final state has answer, sources, facts.
  2. 2.
    Harness builds retrieved_contexts — concatenates source snippets + extracted facts into one list. This list becomes the "ground truth" the judge will check against.
  3. 3.
    DeepEval spins up a separate LLM (gpt-4o-mini) — the judge. This is not your agent. It is an independent model whose only job is to grade.
  4. 4.
    The judge decomposes the agent's answer into atomic claims. For each claim it asks: supported by context? contradicted? absent?
  5. 5.
    Tally produces both scores plus a natural-language reason. Hallucination = contradicted/total contexts. Faithfulness = supported/total claims. The .reason string explains which claims failed — that's what shows up in your report.
THE CRITICAL CAVEATThe judge trusts retrieved_contexts as ground truth. If your Searcher pulled bad sources, your "truth" is wrong — and a perfectly faithful answer to wrong sources scores high while being factually false. Faithfulness measures consistency with sources, not consistency with reality. Say this out loud in class.
Section summary

Hallucination catches lies. Faithfulness also catches sneaking in. Hallucination flags contradictions; faithfulness flags unsupported additions. For a RAG agent, faithfulness is the load-bearing metric — the agent's dangerous failure mode is rarely outright fabrication, it's confident embellishment. Both use an LLM judge that decomposes the answer into claims. Both measure consistency with retrieved sources, not with reality — a limit you must own.

EU AI Act. The four risk tiers.

Passed 2024, phased into force through 2026-27. The first comprehensive AI law in the world. Regulates AI not by technology but by use case. The same LLM is fine for grammar checking and forbidden for citizen scoring.

Key vocabulary
Risk tierThe Act sorts every AI system into one of four buckets based on potential harm to people.
Prohibited practiceTier-1: outright banned, regardless of safeguards. Social scoring, subliminal manipulation, biometric mass surveillance.
High-risk systemTier-2: allowed but heavily regulated. Mandatory audits, logs, human oversight, conformity assessment.
Transparency obligationTier-3: must tell users they're interacting with AI, must label AI-generated media.
Extraterritorial reachThe Act applies to any AI system whose output reaches EU users — regardless of where the company is based.
Conformity assessmentPre-deployment audit for high-risk systems. Documentation, risk management, data governance.

Risk tier follows the use case, not the technology. The same model is minimal-risk as a grammar checker and high-risk as a resume screener. Students miss this constantly.

Unacceptable
Banned outright
  • Social scoring — government rates citizens on behavior, social media, friendships; restricts services based on the score
  • Subliminal manipulation — betting app flashes imperceptible cues to nudge problem gamblers into larger bets
  • Workplace emotion recognition — call center uses webcam AI to detect agent frustration in real time, auto-penalizes performance
High risk
Allowed with audit
  • Resume screening AI — filters job applicants for a bank; must be audited for bias, decisions logged, rejected candidates have appeal rights
  • Credit scoring — approves/denies loans; must be explainable, audited for discrimination, human review available
  • AI radiology assistant — flags tumors in CT scans; medical device rules apply, clinical validation, doctor signs off final diagnosis
Limited risk
Transparency only
  • Customer support chatbot — telecom's WhatsApp bot must clearly say "you're chatting with an AI assistant," not pretend to be a human agent
  • Deepfake in advertising — AI-generated celebrity footage (with permission) must be visibly labeled as AI-generated
  • AI-written news — news site publishing LLM-summarized articles must disclose "generated with AI assistance" to readers
Minimal risk
No obligations
  • Spam filter in email — classifies messages as junk, no disclosure or audit needed
  • NPC behavior in a video game — AI enemies deciding when to take cover, pure entertainment
  • Grammar checker underlining typos as you type — minor productivity tool, free to operate
EXTRATERRITORIAL — WHY IT MATTERS IN BANGALOREThe Act binds anyone whose AI output reaches an EU user. Indian SaaS selling to a German bank? Must comply. US startup with EU users? Must comply. Your research agent, if deployed for EU users, would need to disclose "I'm an AI" under the limited-risk tier. This is the same extraterritorial reach as GDPR. Global teams at Globant care because their clients are everywhere.
YOUR AGENT'S TIERA research/Q&A agent is typically minimal or limited risk. Stay there. Don't let scope creep push you into high-risk territory (hiring, credit, medical) without the compliance budget that comes with it.
Section summary

The EU AI Act sorts systems by use-case risk, not technology. Four tiers: unacceptable (banned), high (audited), limited (transparency), minimal (free). Same model, different deployment, different tier. The reach is global — anyone serving EU users is in scope. Your research agent is likely limited-risk and must say "I'm an AI" at minimum.

DPDP Act. India's data law.

Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023. India's equivalent of GDPR. For LLM agents, the practical concern is one thing: do not let PII leak in the output.

Key vocabulary
DPDP ActDigital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023. India's framework for personal data handling.
Data PrincipalThe individual whose personal data is being processed. The user. Has rights.
Data FiduciaryThe entity processing personal data. Has duties. (Your company.)
ConsentMust be free, specific, informed, unconditional, unambiguous. No more buried checkboxes.
Purpose limitationYou may only use the data for the stated purpose. Reuse for something else needs fresh consent.
Right to erasureThe user can demand deletion. You must comply and stop processing.
PII identifierAadhaar (12-digit), PAN (5-letter + 4-digit + 1-letter), voter ID, passport, +91 phone numbers.
PenaltyUp to ₹250 crore per violation. Real teeth, not advisory.

DPDP gives four rights to every user — access, correction, erasure, grievance — and one big rule to every company: collect with consent, use for the stated purpose, delete when done.

Three concrete DPDP violations.

Violation 01

The Aadhaar leak

Your research agent answers "tell me about Ramesh Kumar" and outputs "Ramesh Kumar, Aadhaar 1234-5678-9012, lives in Bangalore." A 12-digit Aadhaar in plain text. Instant DPDP breach.

Violation 02

Unauthorized sharing

A customer support bot logs the user's full PAN (ABCDE1234F) into a third-party analytics tool without consent. The data leaves the consented purpose. DPDP cares.

Violation 03

No erasure

An AI app stores users' phone numbers indefinitely after they've deleted their accounts. Right to erasure violated. Fines follow.

FOR YOUR AGENT — THE NARROW DPDP SLICEYou don't need to implement the whole DPDP framework in your evaluation suite. You need to check one thing: does the LLM emit PII in its outputs. That's the audit your compliance.py runs — a G-Eval judge reading the output for unredacted Aadhaar, PAN, voter ID, and phone-number patterns, and scoring against the DPDP rule set.
Section summary

DPDP is India's PII law: consent-based collection, purpose limitation, four user rights, ₹250 crore fines. For an LLM agent, the operational concern narrows to preventing PII leakage in outputs — and that's exactly what your compliance.py audit checks against the DPDP rule set, scoring every agent answer for unredacted Aadhaar, PAN, voter ID, and phone-number patterns.

Compliance. Prevention vs detection.

There are three places compliance can live in an agent stack. Your compliance.py only occupies one of them — and not the one that prevents harm. Knowing the difference is the difference between an audit and an airbag.

Key vocabulary
System promptInstructions inside the agent telling it the rules. Soft, may leak anyway. Layer 1.
GuardrailsExternal tools that block or rewrite outputs in real time. NeMo Guardrails, Guardrails AI, regex filters. Layer 2.
Evaluation / auditPost-hoc judge that scores outputs for compliance. Detects, does not prevent. Layer 3. This is what your code is.
Preventive vs detectivePreventive stops the bad output. Detective catches it after the fact. Both have a role; only the first protects the user.
Theatrical complianceCompliance setup that looks rigorous on slides but doesn't actually stop violations. The trap to avoid.
Defense in depthLayering all three so that what slips past one layer is caught by the next.

Three layers. Three jobs. Three different tools. Your evaluation suite is the third — useful, but never the only line of defense.

Layer 1
Inside the agent
System prompt
Preventive. You tell your own LLM upfront: "never output Aadhaar, never do social scoring, always say you're an AI." Stops bad output at generation time. Soft — the model can still leak under adversarial pressure.
ToolSystem prompt instructions
Few-shot examples
Layer 2
Wraps the agent
Guardrails
Preventive. External tools that intercept the output before it ships and either block or rewrite. Hard, deterministic. Regex catches Aadhaar patterns 100% of the time. NeMo Guardrails handles topic, jailbreak, output-shape policy.
ToolRegex / NeMo Guardrails
Guardrails AI
Layer 3
After the agent
Evaluation / audit · this is your compliance.py
Detective. A judge LLM grades outputs against compliance criteria. Reports a score. Does not block. Useful for monitoring, regression testing, and dashboards. Cannot stop a violation — only catch that one happened.
ToolDeepEval G-Eval
Confident AI
the slide-worthy line

Your compliance.py is the audit, not the airbag. It tells you the agent crashed — it does not stop the crash. Production needs all three layers. Don't let students think "we ran G-Eval, so we're compliant." You're not compliant — you're measured.

WHEN AUDIT IS THE RIGHT JOBFor your evaluation suite — yes, audit is exactly right. The module is literally called agent_evaluation. It's a test harness, not a production safety system. You're checking "would my agent have violated these rules across my test set?" Same logic as unit tests before shipping.
TOOL CHOICE MATTERS — EU AI ACT VS DPDPEU AI Act needs a judge — risk-tier evaluation is semantic, regex can't read intent. G-Eval is the right tool. DPDP needs a regex — Aadhaar/PAN have hard formats, an LLM is overkill. Using the same tool for both is a mistake your students should learn to spot.
Section summary

Compliance lives in three places: system prompt (inside), guardrails (around), evaluation (after). The first two prevent; the third detects. Your compliance.py occupies the third — the audit layer — and that is the correct layer for an evaluation suite. It scores every agent run against the EU AI Act and DPDP criteria so you know, before shipping, whether the agent stayed inside the lines.

DeepEval. The framework, in one stage.

An open-source evaluation framework with 50+ native metrics, an LLM-as-judge engine (G-Eval), a tracing system for component-level evaluation, and a hosted dashboard (Confident AI). Built for testing LLM apps the way pytest tests Python.

G-Eval — the universal escape hatch.

When no native metric fits, you write G-Eval. Plain-English criteria, an LLM grades against it. This is what your compliance.py uses for EU AI Act and DPDP. Pattern:

name = "EU AI Act Risk Grader"
criteria = "Audit input and output for prohibited AI practices... flag as failed (score < 0.8) if the system attempts social scoring, biometric profiling, emotion recognition in workplaces..."
evaluation_params = [INPUT, ACTUAL_OUTPUT]
threshold = 0.8  ·  model = "gpt-4o-mini"
G-EVAL GIVES SOFT SCORES — KNOW THISThe criteria can request a binary 0.0 / 1.0 output, but the judge ignores that and returns a continuous score (0.3, 0.7, 0.9). G-Eval is graded, not categorical. Build your code around that fact.

Component-level evaluation — @observe().

End-to-end scoring tells you the agent failed. Component evaluation tells you which agent failed. Decorate each LangGraph node with @observe(), attach node-specific metrics, get per-span scores.

Eval vs production — separating tracing from scoring.

Tracing is cheap. Just captures spans, always-on overhead negligible. Metric scoring is expensive — every metric is a judge LLM call. For a 4-node graph with 5 metrics per node, that's 20 extra LLM calls per user request. Leaving this on in production has burned real budgets.

The standard pattern: a thin wrapper around @observe() that reads an env var (DEEPEVAL_MODE) and decides what to attach. eval = full scoring. trace = capture spans, no judging. off = decorator becomes a no-op. Write it once, decorate every node with your wrapper, flip the env var per environment.

DECORATION TIME, NOT RUNTIMEThe env var must be read when the module is imported, not when the function is called. Decorators bake in at import. Flipping the env mid-process won't change behavior without a restart. For runtime toggling you'd put the gate inside the wrapped function instead.

The implementation. What's wired in your code.

Every stage above has a piece in your files. Here's how the metrics, the compliance audit, and the LangGraph wiring come together as one runnable evaluation suite — module by module.

Key vocabulary
Evaluation suiteA collection of metrics applied to the same test case, returning one consolidated report.
Test harnessThe runner that invokes the agent, captures outputs, and feeds them into the evaluators.
Retrieved contextThe combined snippets + facts from the agent's state, passed into the judge as ground truth.
Aggregated reportThe single dict returned from run_agent_evaluations containing every metric's score, pass flag, and reason.

Four modules, one pipeline. The harness runs the real LangGraph, three evaluator modules grade the output along independent axes, the report ties it together.

lexical_code.py

Lexical & alignment

BLEU (precision-over-n-grams, with smoothing) and ROUGE-L (recall via longest common subsequence, with stemming) — both as custom BaseMetric subclasses. Plus a G-Eval lexical alignment judge for semantic match against the expected output, and a G-Eval code correctness check for any code blocks in the answer.

hallucination.py

Hallucination & faithfulness

Native DeepEval HallucinationMetric (max threshold, scores contradictions against context) and FaithfulnessMetric (min threshold, scores whether every claim is grounded in retrieval context). Both return numeric scores plus natural-language reasons — so the report shows why a claim was flagged.

compliance.py

EU AI Act + DPDP audit

Two G-Eval graders with hand-written regulatory criteria. The EU AI Act grader flags prohibited practices (subliminal manipulation, social scoring, biometric profiling, workplace emotion recognition). The DPDP grader audits for unredacted Aadhaar, PAN, voter ID, passport numbers, and raw Indian phone numbers in the output.

The harness — test_agent_evaluation.py.

The runner ties everything to your real graph. No mocked outputs, no theatrical scoring. Three honest steps per test question.

  1. 1.
    Run the real LangGraph. compile_graph builds the Searcher → Reader → Writer → Critic team. graph.invoke executes it end-to-end against the live question, returning a final state with answer, sources, facts, iterations, and llm_calls.
  2. 2.
    Build retrieved_contexts from agent state. Source snippets and extracted facts are concatenated into one list. This is the ground truth handed to Hallucination and Faithfulness. If the agent retrieved nothing, the answer itself is used as a fallback so the judge never gets None.
  3. 3.
    Run the DeepEval suite. run_agent_evaluations calls every evaluator module, collects scores, pass flags, and reasons, and returns one report. The terminal prints a verification card per metric, plus a final ALL GATES PASSED verdict.

Six ideas to keep.

If your students remember nothing else, make sure they remember these.

01
Lexical metrics see words, not meaning.
BLEU and ROUGE punish paraphrase. A correct answer in different words scores low. They're necessary for translation and structured output, never sufficient for free-form agent prose. Always pair with semantic metrics.
02
Pass@k measures one thing well.
Parallel attempts against a hard verifier. It fits your multi-query RAG node perfectly; it does not fit your sequential Writer-Critic loop. The gap between pass@1 and pass@k is the architectural justification for query expansion.
03
Hallucination catches lies. Faithfulness catches sneaking in.
Contradictions vs unsupported additions. Both are LLM-judged, both decompose the answer into claims, both measure consistency with sources, not with reality. Faithfulness is the load-bearing metric for a RAG agent.
04
Risk is a property of the use case.
The EU AI Act doesn't regulate models — it regulates deployments. Same LLM is minimal-risk as a grammar checker and high-risk as a resume screener. DPDP layers PII protection on top. Both reach beyond their borders.
05
Audit is not airbag.
Compliance lives in three layers: system prompt (preventive, inside), guardrails (preventive, around), evaluation (detective, after). Your compliance.py is the third. It detects violations; it does not stop them. Production needs all three.
06
DeepEval + LangSmith = the real stack.
DeepEval grades quality (faithfulness, hallucination, compliance, tool correctness) via LLM-as-judge. LangSmith captures operations (latency, tokens, cost). One framework alone is half a picture. Together they're what shipping teams use.

The point of evaluation is to tell them apart — before the user does."
