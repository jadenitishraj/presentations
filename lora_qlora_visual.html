03

36 wild sentences.

Same template every time: the [subject] sat on the [surprise]. Variety in subjects and endings teaches the abstract pattern, not the specific examples.

Production fine-tunes use thousands of examples. We use 36. It's enough to demonstrate the technique cleanly — the model has already seen billions of words during pre-training, so we're nudging existing knowledge, not teaching from scratch.

05

The big idea: freeze + adapt.

Don't train the 1 billion existing weights. Freeze them. Add tiny new weight matrices on a few specific layers. Train only those.

q_proj1152 × 1152+ adapter
k_proj1152 × 256untouched
v_proj1152 × 256+ adapter
o_proj1152 × 1152untouched
gate_proj1152 × 6912untouched
up_proj1152 × 6912untouched
down_proj6912 × 1152untouched
×26 layers · only q_proj & v_proj per layer get adapters
Think of it like editing a book.

Without LoRA, you rewrite every page. 1 billion edits.

With LoRA, you stick small sticky notes on a few important pages. The book stays the same. Your edits live on the notes.

Only ~745,000 numbers get trained. 0.07% of the model.

The LoRA trick: low-rank decomposition.

A full weight update would be a giant matrix. LoRA approximates it with two tiny matrices that multiply together. Same shape, fraction of the numbers.

Per layer: ~1.3M numbers → 18,432 numbers.

A full 1152×1152 update would have ~1.3 million parameters. The decomposition 1152×8 + 8×1152 has only 18,432. ~70× compression. The bottleneck dimension r=8 is where the magic happens — the model only needs 8 dimensions of "wiggle room" to learn the new task.
During the forward pass:
output = W₀ × x + (α/r) × A × B × x
frozen base trainable adapter
07

Where do the adapters go?

Inside each transformer block, on the Q and V projection matrices. Same Wq and Wv we built by hand in MiniGPT.

The original LoRA paper (Hu et al., 2021) tested adapting every linear layer. They found adapting just q_proj and v_proj is enough for almost any task.

Why these two? Q decides "what to look for" and V carries "what to deliver." Tweak those, and you steer attention's behavior. K and the FFN can stay frozen — they're already general-purpose machinery.

In Gemma 3 1B: 26 transformer blocks × 2 adapter pairs = 52 adapter modules. Each one is 2 small matrices.
08

The six knobs of LoRA.

Six settings determine everything. Most of the time, you only really tune two: rank and alpha.

r
8
Rank — the bottleneck. How many "hidden dimensions" of change. Common: 4, 8, 16, 32. Higher = more capacity, more params.
lora_alpha
16
Volume knob. The adapter's contribution is multiplied by α/r. Rule of thumb: α = 2 × r.
target_modules
["q_proj", "v_proj"]
Which layers get adapters. Just Q and V is enough for most tasks. Add more for harder problems.
lora_dropout
0.05
Randomly zeros 5% of adapter activations during training. Prevents overfitting on tiny datasets.
bias
"none"
Don't train any bias parameters. Biases rarely matter much — skip them, save params.
task_type
"CAUSAL_LM"
Tells PEFT this is a next-token-prediction model (like GPT). Sets up adapters correctly for this task.
apply_lora.py PEFT · LoraConfig
from peft import LoraConfig, get_peft_model lora_config = LoraConfig( r=8, lora_alpha=16, target_modules=["q_proj", "v_proj"], lora_dropout=0.05, bias="none", task_type="CAUSAL_LM", ) model = get_peft_model(model, lora_config) model.print_trainable_parameters() # trainable params: 745,472 || all: 1B || 0.07%
get_peft_model(...) The magic line. PEFT walks every layer, finds q_proj and v_proj, freezes them, attaches new A and B matrices on top. Returns a wrapped model that behaves identically but has only adapter parameters trainable.
print_trainable_parameters() Quick sanity check. You should see ~0.07% trainable. If it says 100%, something went wrong — the adapters aren't attached.
Why r=8 specifically? Empirically, language models need surprisingly few dimensions of adjustment for new tasks. The original paper showed r=1 even works for some tasks. r=8 is the safe sweet spot.

10

Train the adapters.

Same training loop you saw in MiniGPT — forward, loss, backward, step. Only difference: gradients only flow into the 745k adapter weights. Everything else stays still.

SFTTrainer from the TRL library handles everything: batching, tokenization, loss computation, backprop, optimizer steps. We don't write the training loop ourselves.

Key parameters:
• learning_rate=2e-4 — standard for LoRA
• num_train_epochs=20 — small dataset → more epochs
• batch_size=4 — fits comfortably in T4
• lr_scheduler=cosine — smoothly decay LR
• fp16=True — mixed precision for speed
Training loss · 100 steps · loss dropped from 7.86 → 2.01
train_lora.py SFTTrainer · TRL
from trl import SFTTrainer, SFTConfig training_args = SFTConfig( output_dir="./gemma-1b-lora", num_train_epochs=20, per_device_train_batch_size=4, gradient_accumulation_steps=2, learning_rate=2e-4, warmup_ratio=0.1, lr_scheduler_type="cosine", logging_steps=5, save_strategy="no", fp16=True, max_length=64, dataset_text_field="text", ) trainer = SFTTrainer( model=model, args=training_args, train_dataset=dataset, processing_class=tokenizer, ) trainer.train()
num_train_epochs = 20 Loop through the 36 sentences 20 times. Production fine-tunes on millions of examples use just 1–3 epochs. We use more because our dataset is tiny.
batch_size=4 + grad_accum=2 Effective batch = 4 × 2 = 8. Compute small batches, accumulate gradients across them, then step. Simulates a bigger batch without using more memory.
learning_rate = 2e-4 The standard LR for LoRA fine-tuning. Higher than full fine-tuning because we're updating fewer params. Works for almost any task.
warmup_ratio + cosine scheduler Ramp LR from 0 up to 2e-4 over the first 10% of steps, then smoothly decay back down. Keeps adapter weights from exploding early when they're random.
processing_class = tokenizer Tells TRL to use this exact tokenizer. Without it, newer TRL versions try to auto-detect and fail because Gemma 3 has both text and image variants.
trainer.train() Single line runs the entire loop. Watch the loss drop from ~7.8 to ~2.0 over 100 steps. That's the model learning the wild pattern.

13

Now QLoRA: shrink the base too.

If we're never training the frozen base, why store it in 16 bits? Compress it to 4 bits. The trainable adapters stay full precision.

16 bits
0.4732891234...
FP16 storage · 2 bytes per weight
→
4 bits
1110
NF4 storage · 0.5 bytes per weight
4× compression on the frozen base.

Each weight loses some precision. But these weights aren't being updated anyway — they're frozen. The tiny precision loss is invisible for most tasks. The adapters stay in BF16 because they need full precision to learn.
14

How 4-bit quantization works.

4 bits = 16 possible values. The trick: place those 16 values where weights actually cluster, not uniformly.

Neural network weights follow a bell curve · most values near zero
● FP4 — uniform spacing (worse)
● NF4 — clustered near zero (better)
qlora_config.py BitsAndBytesConfig
from transformers import BitsAndBytesConfig bnb_config = BitsAndBytesConfig( load_in_4bit=True, bnb_4bit_quant_type="nf4", bnb_4bit_compute_dtype=torch.bfloat16, bnb_4bit_use_double_quant=True, )
load_in_4bit = True Master switch. Tells transformers to delegate weight loading to bitsandbytes, which quantizes each weight from FP16 to 4 bits as it's read from disk.
bnb_4bit_quant_type = "nf4" "NormalFloat 4." Places its 16 representable values where weights actually cluster (bell curve). Better than uniform "fp4" for neural networks.
bnb_4bit_compute_dtype = bfloat16 "Store in 4 bits, but when doing math, temporarily upcast to BF16." Memory savings of 4-bit storage + precision of BF16 arithmetic.
bnb_4bit_use_double_quant = True Quantizes the quantization scales themselves. Saves another ~0.4 bits per parameter. Tiny accuracy cost, noticeable memory win on huge models.

QLoRA = quantized base + LoRA adapters.

One line of code change from regular LoRA. Same training loop. Same hyperparameters. Just a quantization config slipped in.

LoRA · Part 1
🧊 Base Gemma → FP16
➕ LoRA adapters → FP16
🎓 Training math → FP16
Memory: 1.86 GB base + adapters
QLoRA · Part 2
🧊 Base Gemma → NF4 (4-bit)
➕ LoRA adapters → BF16
🎓 Training math → BF16 (upcast on the fly)
Memory: 0.91 GB base + adapters · ↓ 51%
load_qlora.py The only line that changes
from peft import prepare_model_for_kbit_training model_4b = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained( model_id_4b, quantization_config=bnb_config, # ← only new line device_map="auto", ) model_4b = prepare_model_for_kbit_training(model_4b) # Same LoraConfig as Part 1 — unchanged model_4b = get_peft_model(model_4b, lora_config_4b) # Trainer config: bf16=True instead of fp16=True training_args_4b = SFTConfig( ..., bf16=True, # bf16 plays nice with 4-bit per_device_train_batch_size=2, gradient_accumulation_steps=4, )
quantization_config = bnb_config The whole story in one line. Hand transformers the BnB config, and weights load in 4-bit instead of FP16. Everything downstream still works.
prepare_model_for_kbit_training Helper that fixes 4-bit training quirks: enables gradient checkpointing, upcasts LayerNorm to FP32 for stability, ensures gradients flow through the model correctly. Skip this and training fails mysteriously.
bf16=True (not fp16) BFloat16 has the same range as FP32 with reduced precision. Plays better with 4-bit storage than FP16. Mixing FP16 with 4-bit causes numerical instability.
batch_size=2, accum=4 4-bit weights need temporary upcasting during backward pass = extra memory spike. Drop batch size to be safe, bump grad_accum to keep effective batch the same (2×4 = 8).
LoraConfig is identical Same r=8, alpha=16, q_proj+v_proj. The whole point of QLoRA: quantization is invisible to the trainer. LoRA setup doesn't change at all.

Why it matters at scale.

For our 1B model, the savings look modest — about 1 GB. But the percentage holds at any scale. For a 70-billion parameter model:

FP16 loading: 140 GB · needs a $30K server cluster
QLoRA 4-bit: 35 GB · fits on a single A100 or two RTX 4090s

That's the difference between "research lab only" and "anyone with a gaming PC can fine-tune Llama 70B." That's why QLoRA democratized fine-tuning.

Three lessons.

If your students remember nothing else from this poster, make sure they remember this.

01
LoRA freezes the base.
Add tiny A·B adapter pairs on Q and V. Train only those. 0.07% of the parameters do the work of teaching the model a new task.
02
QLoRA compresses the frozen base.
If we're never updating it, store it in 4 bits instead of 16. NF4 places the 16 quantization values where weights actually cluster. 4× compression, almost no quality loss.
03
Adapters are tiny and swappable.
2.86 MB per adapter vs 2 GB for the base. Host one base model, ship dozens of skill packs. This is how production LLM serving works at scale.
